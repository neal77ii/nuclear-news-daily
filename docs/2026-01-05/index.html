우크라이나 분쟁 속 자포리자 원자력 발전소의 미래

2025년 12월 29일 발행

요약

  • 카테고리: 현황
  • 러시아와 우크라이나 간 자포리자 원자력 발전소를 둘러싼 갈등 지속
  • 젤렌스키(Volodymyr Zelenskiy)와 트럼프(Donald Trump) 간 발전소 운영에 대한 협상 진행 중
  • 러시아는 발전소 재가동 및 전력망 연결 계획
  • 국제원자력기구(IAEA)는 발전소 주변 전쟁으로 인한 안전 위험 경고

배경

자포리자 원자력 발전소는 유럽 최대 규모의 원자력 발전소로, 현재 러시아가 통제하고 있습니다. 2022년 3월 러시아가 점령한 이후 발전소는 전기 생산을 중단한 상태입니다.

현재 상황

  • 발전소 위치: 드니프로 강변의 에네르호다르 시
  • 6개의 소비에트 설계 원자로, 총 5.7 기가와트 용량
  • 현재 외부 전력 공급에 의존하여 핵 물질 냉각 유지

러시아의 목적

러시아는 다음과 같은 목적으로 발전소 통제를 원하고 있습니다:

  • 러시아 남부 지역의 에너지 부족 해소
  • 전력 생산 재개 계획
  • 2027년 중반까지 발전소 재가동 가능성 언급

우크라이나의 입장

우크라이나는 러시아의 발전소 점령에 대해 다음과 같은 입장입니다:

  • 발전소 운영권 회복 희망
  • 미국과 공동 운영 제안
  • 발전소 복구에는 5-7년 소요 예상

냉각 시스템 문제

카호프카 수력발전댐 파괴로 인한 심각한 문제:

  • 원자로 냉각수 부족

듀크 에너지(Duke Energy), NRC에 조기 부지 허가 신청

원본: https://www.ans.org/news/2026-01-05/article-7636/duke-energy-submits-an-esp-application-to-the-nrc/

듀크 에너지(Duke Energy), NRC에 조기 부지 허가 신청

2026년 1월 5일, 월요일

요약

  • 카테고리: 현황 (원자력 투자)
  • 듀크 에너지, 북캐롤라이나주 벨루스 크릭 부지에 새로운 원자력 발전소 조기 부지 허가 신청
  • 6개의 잠재적 원자력 반응로 기술 포함, 2037년까지 600MW 원자력 발전소 계획
  • 첫 소형모듈원자로(SMR)는 2036년 가동 예정

듀크 에너지(Duke Energy)는 2025년 10월 발표한 원자력 투자 계획에 따라, 북캐롤라이나주 스토크스 카운티의 벨루스 크릭 스팀 발전소 인근 부지에 대한 조기 부지 허가(ESP) 신청서를 원자력규제위원회(NRC)에 제출했습니다.

주요 세부 내용

"우리는 새로운 원자력 개발에 대해 위험을 줄이고 기술 성숙도를 고려하는 전략적 접근 방식을 취하고 있습니다." - 켈빈 헨더슨(Kelvin Henderson) 듀크 에너지 원자력 책임자

듀크 에너지의 ESP 신청은 기술 중립적이며, 이는 최종 원자로 설계를 선택하기 전에 NRC 허가를 받을 수 있음을 의미합니다. 신청서에는 4개의 소형모듈원자로(SMR) 설계와 2개의 비경수로 설계 등 총 6개의 잠재적 원자로 기술이 포함되어 있습니다.

향후 계획

회사는 2037년까지 해당 부지에 600메가와트의 새로운 원자력 발전소를 가동할 계획이며, 첫 번째 SMR은 2036년에 가동될 예정입니다. NRC 프로젝트 개요에 따르면, 현재 약 300MWe의 총 전기 출력을 가진 SMR과 협의 중입니다.

AI 전문가 분석

이번 듀크 에너지의 조기 부지 허가 신청은 미국 원자력 산업의 새로운 전환점을 나타냅니다. 소형모듈원자로 기술의 발전과 에너지 전환에 대한 전략적 접근은 향후 원자력 에너지 산업의 중요한 트렌드를 보여줍니다. 이는 탄소 중립과 에너지 안보 측면에서 매우 중요한 시도로 평가됩니다.

2025년 원자력 발전의 시끄럽지만 조용한 한 해

2024년 12월 24일

요약

  • 카테고리: 현황
  • 2025년 글로벌 원자력 발전 설비 용량이 전체적으로 감소
  • 새로 가동된 원자로(1.8GW)보다 폐쇄된 원자로(2.8GW)가 더 많음
  • 세계적으로 417개 원자로가 337GW의 전력을 생산 중

전 세계 원자력 발전소와 관련된 보도 자료, 정책 변화, 그리고 신규 건설 약속에도 불구하고 2025년은 실제 원자로 추가 측면에서는 그다지 성공적이지 않았습니다.

BloombergNEF의 새로운 데이터에 따르면, 세계적으로 올해 더 많은 기가와트 규모의 원자로가 폐쇄되었습니다. 12월 1일까지 11개월 동안 단 2개의 새로운 원자로만 가동되었으며, 총 1.8GW의 용량을 차지했습니다. 반면에 7개 원자로(총 2.8GW 용량)가 영구적으로 폐쇄되었습니다.

국가별 현황

벨기에는 2003년 원자력 단계적 폐지 법안을 폐기하기로 결정했음에도 불구하고 2개의 원자로를 폐쇄했습니다. 대만은 마안산 원전의 마지막 원자로를 폐쇄하며 원자력 에너지에서 완전히 철수했습니다.

향후 전망

전문가들은 2026년이 적어도 15년 만에 처음으로 전 세계적으로 폐쇄 예정인 원자로가 없는 해가 될 것으로 전망하고 있습니다. 2027년 폐쇄가 다시 시작되겠지만, 2029년까지 새로운 설비 용량이 폐쇄를 크게 앞지를 것으로 예상됩니다.

미국의 역할

주요 프로젝트

헬리온 에너지(Helion Energy)의 핵융합 원자로

헬리온 에너지는 말라가(Malaga)에 50메가와트 규모의 오리온(Orion) 핵융합 원자로를 건설 중입니다. 이 프로젝트는 마이크로소프트와의 전력 구매 계약을 통해 2028년까지 전력을 공급할 예정입니다.

아마존은 X-에너지와 협력하여 3억 3,400만 달러를 투자해 4개의 소형 모듈형 원자로를 개발할 계획입니다. 이 프로젝트는 최대 960메가와트의 전력 생산을 목표로 합니다.

정책적 지원

What Plans Are Being Discussed?

Russia took control of the plant, which lies close to the front lines, in March 2022 and announced plans to connect it to its power grid.

Almost all countries consider that it belongs to Ukraine, but Russia says it is owned by Russia and a unit of Russia's state-owned Rosatom nuclear corporation runs the plant.

Zelenskiy stated in December that the U.S. had proposed joint trilateral operation of the nuclear power plant with an American chief manager.

Current Status

Key details:

  • Total capacity of 5.7 gigawatts

Duke Energy Submits an ESP Application to the NRC

Published: Mon, Jan 5, 2026

TL;DR

  • Duke Energy submitted an Early Site Permit (ESP) application to the NRC for a potential new nuclear site near Belews Creek Steam Station
  • The application is technology-neutral, including six potential reactor technologies (four SMRs and two non-light-water designs)
  • Duke plans to have 600 megawatts of new nuclear power online by 2037, with the first SMR expected in 2036

Following up on an October announcement on plans to invest more heavily in nuclear power, Duke Energy closed out 2025 by submitting an early site permit application to the Nuclear Regulatory Commission. This ESP application is for a site near the Belews Creek Steam Station, a coal and natural gas plant in Stokes County, N.C., where Duke has been pursuing a new nuclear project for two years.

Application Details

Duke's ESP application is technology neutral, meaning the company will be able to receive an NRC permit before making a final reactor design selection. According to Duke, the application contains six potential reactor technologies, "including four small modular reactor designs and two non–light-water designs." The application does not include large light water reactor designs like the 11 units Duke already operates in the Carolinas.

The company added that it plans to have 600 megawatts of new nuclear power on line at the site by 2037, with the first SMR coming on line in 2036. The NRC's project overview states that it is currently engaged with Duke on an SMR "rated at approximately 300 MWe gross electrical output." A second, similarly sized reactor would get Duke to the 600 MWe of nuclear power planned for Belews Creek.

Interestingly, the NRC's project overview also specifies that the project involves an LWR, despite Duke saying that two of its potential six technologies are non-LWR designs.

Future Considerations

Despite the submission of an ESP application, the company noted that it "has yet to make a decision to build new nuclear units." This is Duke's first ESP, and the company described the optional NRC process as a risk-mitigation strategy that will allow it to resolve environmental and site safety topics early in the project.

"We're taking a strategic approach to new nuclear development that allows us to advance licensing activities while reducing risks and allowing technologies to mature," added Duke Energy chief nuclear officer Kelvin Henderson.

AI Analysis

The submission of this ESP application represents a significant step in Duke Energy's nuclear expansion strategy. It demonstrates the company's commitment to exploring advanced nuclear technologies while maintaining flexibility in reactor selection. The technology-neutral approach allows Duke to evaluate emerging reactor designs and potentially incorporate the most promising options for future deployment.

Nuclear Power's Loud-but-Quiet Year

24 December 2025

TL;DR

  • Global nuclear capacity decreased in 2025, with more reactor retirements than additions
  • Only two new reactors came online, while seven were permanently shuttered
  • Despite current challenges, the nuclear power sector may see a rebound in the coming years

For press releases, policy changes, and promises to build new nuclear power, 2025 was a gangbusters year. For actually adding new reactors to the grid, not so much.

In fact, around the world, more gigawatts' worth of nuclear reactors were retired than turned on this year, according to new data from the consultancy BloombergNEF.

In the 11 months leading up to Dec. 1, only two new reactors came online, totaling 1.8GW. Meanwhile, seven reactors totaling 2.8GW of capacity were permanently shuttered. The net effect? Global nuclear operating capacity declined by just over 1GW. Overall, the world had 417 reactors in operation churning out 337GW of power as of the start of this month.

Key Developments

Belgium led the retreat, shuttering two reactors this year, even as the country's lawmakers voted in May to repeal a 2003 law that required the country to phase out nuclear power entirely.

Taiwan also contributed to the decline when it closed the last reactor at its Maanshan plant on the island's southern tip, completing the country's long-awaited exit from atomic energy. Russia will round out the closures by decommissioning three 12-megawatt units at a plant in the Arctic by the end of this month.

Future Outlook

The shutdowns are the result of a yearslong pullback on nuclear power across much of the world, with China and Russia being the key exceptions.

But they also come at what may be a turning point for that global retreat from nuclear. Around the world, new technologies are racing toward maturity, shuttered reactors are being revived, and dealmakers are seeking to shore up the future supply of clean electricity by investing in new nuclear power.

The nuclear power sector appears to be at a critical juncture, balancing between declining capacity and potential technological innovations. Idaho Lab Produces First Fuel for Fast Molten Salt Reactor, Paving Way for Maritime Deployment

Published: January 4, 2026

TL;DR

  • Scientists at Idaho National Laboratory (INL) have successfully produced the first chloride-based molten salt fuel for a fast reactor
  • The Molten Chloride Reactor Experiment (MCRE) aims to demonstrate advanced nuclear technologies for maritime applications
  • This breakthrough represents a significant milestone in U.S. nuclear energy innovation, with potential for more efficient and safer nuclear power

Scientists at the Idaho National Laboratory (INL) have achieved a historic breakthrough in nuclear energy by launching full-scale production of enriched fuel salt for the Molten Chloride Reactor Experiment (MCRE)—the world's first test of a molten chloride salt fast reactor.

"This is the first time in history that chloride-based molten salt fuel has been produced for a fast reactor," said Bill Phillips, technical lead for salt synthesis. "It's a major milestone for American innovation and a clear signal of our national commitment to advanced nuclear energy."

Project Overview

The MCRE project is a public-private collaboration between Southern Company, TerraPower, CORE POWER, and the U.S. Department of Energy to demonstrate advanced nuclear technologies, showing promise in the maritime market. Operations are expected to begin as soon as 2030.

Technological Innovation

Unlike traditional reactors that use solid fuel rods and water as a coolant, most molten salt reactors rely on liquid fuel—a mixture of salts containing fissile material. This design allows for:
  • Higher operating temperatures
  • Better fuel efficiency
  • Enhanced safety
  • New applications, including compact nuclear systems for ships and remote installations

Maritime Implications

"The implications for the maritime industry are significant," said Don Wood, senior technical advisor for MCRE. "Molten salt reactors could provide ships with highly efficient, low-maintenance nuclear power, reducing emissions and enabling long-range, uninterrupted travel."

Production Milestone

The fuel salt production process began in 2020. Key achievements include:
  • Initial target: 90% uranium metal conversion, 18 kilograms of fuel salt per batch
  • 2024 breakthrough: 95% conversion and full-batch production
  • Ability to produce a batch in as little as one day

Future Outlook

"This milestone isn't just about producing fuel—it's about proving that the U.S. can lead in next-generation nuclear innovation," said James King, MCRE project director.

No, the United States does not need a costly national cancer study near nuclear reactors

January 2, 2026

TL;DR

  • Large-scale studies of nuclear workers show no statistically significant link between low-dose radiation and cancer risk
  • Proposed national cancer study near nuclear reactors would be extremely costly and unlikely to produce meaningful results
  • Resources would be better spent on addressing known health risks like radon exposure

By Adam Stein and PJ Seel

With the return of nuclear energy to the public square and serious efforts among policymakers to commercialize a new generation of reactors in the United States and Europe, familiar claims about the risks of nuclear reactors have returned as well.

In a recent Bulletin article, Joseph Mangano and Robert Alvarez call for a new national cancer study focused on communities near nuclear power plants before any further expansion of nuclear power is undertaken in the United States. The authors are long-time opponents of nuclear energy and repeat claims based upon decades-old studies and outdated science that not only ignore an enormous body of better and more recent data but also defy contemporary scientific understandings of the health risks associated with low doses of ionizing radiation.

Scientific Consensus

The scientific consensus on this question is clear: The United States does not need a national cancer study near nuclear reactors.

Routine reactor emissions, both activated material and fission by-products, pose no meaningful health risk. Even if some vanishingly small effect existed, it would be statistically indistinguishable from the background cancer rate and would be lost in the noise of lifestyle, environmental, and biological risk factors.

How do we know this? Because large sample studies have tracked the health of millions of workers exposed to routine ionizing radiation and found no definite link to increased risk of cancer at low effective doses—defined as below 0.1 Sievert.

Worker Exposure Monitoring

Workers at nuclear facilities are closely monitored by employers and occupational health authorities. Many wear dosimeters that measure real-time radiation exposure, and their clothing and work environments are regularly tested for radiation contamination.

The Energy Department's Million Person Study has collected and is tracking the most extensive cohort of US nuclear workers and atomic veterans exposed to low-dose radiation throughout the last century. This study provides several orders of magnitude greater statistical power than any community-based population study ever will.

Dose and Risk Context

In 2006, the average American received about 6.2 millisieverts per year from natural background and medical imaging. Living near a nuclear power plant typically adds less than 0.01 millisievert per year—two orders of magnitude lower and comparable to the radiation exposure from a few hours of air travel.

Distinguishing the contribution of such a tiny radiation dose from other cancer contributors is simply not possible. Risk factors such as smoking, radon, air pollution, pesticides, diet, and workplace exposures each individually contribute far more to cancer incidence than routine reactor emissions.

Conclusion

Fifty years of studying millions of nuclear workers and residents near plants have demonstrated that routine nuclear plant operations pose no detectable health risk to the public.

Resources would be better spent on addressing known health risks like radon exposure, which the Environmental Protection Agency estimates causes up to 22,000 lung cancer deaths annually, or building more nuclear reactors to reduce emissions of fine particles and other airborne pollutants.

The authors leverage extensive epidemiological data and research to demonstrate that low-dose radiation from nuclear facilities does not pose a significant cancer risk. Their recommendation to focus resources on more impactful health interventions appears well-reasoned and grounded in current scientific understanding.

Why U.S. Nuclear Licensing Moves So Slowly

Published: January 3, 2026

TL;DR

  • U.S. nuclear power development has significantly lagged behind other nations like China and France
  • Complex regulatory processes and bureaucratic hurdles slow down nuclear reactor licensing
  • Experts propose various reforms to streamline nuclear energy development while maintaining safety standards

Rising Clean-Power Demand and Nuclear Energy

As communities confront the real-world costs of global climate change—from more frequent and more severe weather events to mounting pressure on agricultural systems—climate change advocates continue to press for rapid reductions in greenhouse gas emissions. Fossil-fuel-based electricity generation contributes more than 40 percent of global carbon emissions, making the electric generation sector a critical front for decarbonization.

Nuclear Energy's Potential

Nuclear energy offers one such low-carbon energy source, leading some analysts to contend that nuclear energy is vital to reducing the electricity generation sector's carbon emissions and avoid the worst impacts of global climate change. Nuclear plants generate large quantities of electricity with low direct carbon emissions and provide year-round, consistent baseline energy generation capacity.

Historical Context and Current Challenges

The United States boasts a rich history of nuclear innovation. The world's first commercial-scale nuclear reactor opened in Shippingport, Pennsylvania in December 1957. Despite operating the world's largest nuclear-generation portfolio, the U.S. supplies only 18.2 percent of its electricity from nuclear power, compared to France's 67.3 percent.

Comparative International Development

Since 2000, the United States has brought only three new commercial reactors online—Watts Bar 2 in 2016, Vogtle 3 in 2023, and Vogtle 4 in 2024. In contrast, China has nearly tripled its nuclear energy production and currently has 27 reactors under construction.

Barriers to Nuclear Development

Commentators offer several explanations for the United States' struggles, including:

  • Economic forces like competition from cheap natural gas
  • High financing and construction costs
  • Unresolved long-term waste disposal politics
  • Complex nuclear licensing regulations

Proposed Solutions

Experts have proposed various regulatory reforms to encourage nuclear energy deployment, including:

  • Streamlining licensing processes
  • Clarifying administrative authorities
  • Investing in agency funding and training
  • Improving interagency coordination

AI Model's Perspective

The challenges facing U.S. nuclear energy development reflect a complex interplay of technological, economic, and regulatory factors. While safety remains paramount, the current licensing system appears to create unnecessary barriers to innovation. Balanced reform could help the United States leverage its historical nuclear expertise to address climate change more effectively.

With a Focus on Nuclear Power, Takaichi's Energy Policy Takes Shape

TL;DR

  • Prime Minister Sanae Takaichi is prioritizing nuclear power and nuclear fusion technologies
  • Efforts are being made to restart aging nuclear reactors across Japan
  • Renewable energy, particularly megasolar projects, is receiving less attention under her administration

As she settles into the nation's top office, Prime Minister Sanae Takaichi's energy policy is coming into focus. Conventional nuclear power and the pursuit of futuristic technologies like nuclear fusion are being prioritized, while renewable energy, especially megasolar, is getting less attention.

Over her short two months in office, Takaichi has emphasized the restart of aging nuclear reactors and developing futuristic — but not yet commercially viable — technologies like nuclear fusion. She has also given considerably less attention to renewables, with the exception of geothermal, all with the goal of making Japan 100% energy self-sufficient.

Nuclear Power

Takaichi, long one of Japan's strongest advocates for nuclear power, says the controversial energy source is needed to deal with projected increases in electricity consumption, especially by energy-hungry data centers. But so far she has provided few details as to how much it will cost to restart many of the country's still-dormant nuclear reactors.

Nationwide, Japan has 60 reactors. Of these, 24 are being decommissioned, a process that will take decades. The two dozen being scrapped include 10 in Fukushima Prefecture, where the March 11, 2011, quake and tsunami caused a triple meltdown at Tokyo Electric Power Company Holdings' (Tepco) Fukushima No. 1 nuclear power plant.

Of the remainder, 14 had been restarted as of December. Another four have received permission to restart after beefing up plant safety, while eight are currently undergoing safety inspections after applying for a restart.

Geothermal Energy

When Takaichi signed a coalition agreement in October with the Japan Innovation Party, one of the promises she made was to explore the potential of geothermal energy sources.

The Japan Organization for Metals and Energy Security (JOGMAC) estimates total geothermal potential at around 23 gigawatts, the third largest in the world after the United States and Indonesia. However, Japan ranks 10th worldwide in terms of installed capacity. Geothermal provided only 0.3% of Japan's total electricity in 2023 and that figure is expected to rise to only 1% by 2030, and to 1% to 2% by 2040.

Renewable Energy

The prime minister made headlines with her opposition to megasolar project development in the Hokkaido's Kushiro Wetlands area, and her government is proposing that new operators be excluded from government subsidy programs beginning in fiscal 2027.

Takaichi does not oppose solar energy if it's developed in Japan. During parliamentary questioning, she emphasized that the industry should focus on promoting perovskite solar cells invented in Japan.

Natural Gas and Other Sources

Liquefied natural gas accounted for nearly 30% of Japan's electricity in fiscal 2024. Under Takaichi, efforts are being made to increase Japanese investment in the U.S., which accounted for 8.7% of Japan's 2024 LNG imports.

Urenco USA marks enrichment milestones

Fri, Dec 19, 2025, 1:00PM

TL;DR

  • Urenco USA produced its first enriched uranium above 5% (8.5% U-235)
  • Started a new centrifuge cascade at its National Enrichment Facility
  • Aims to install 700,000 separative work units (SWU) of capacity by 2027

Urenco USA has highlighted the completion of a successful year of advancing nuclear fuel supply in the U.S. by achieving two new milestones this month: The first production of enriched uranium above 5 percent uranium-235, and the startup of the company's next cascade of centrifuges as part of its capacity installation program.

Enriched Uranium Production

UUSA finished its initial production run of uranium enriched to 8.5 percent U-235 on December 11. The company said that this achievement demonstrates its ability to produce low-enriched uranium plus (LEU+), which is uranium enriched to a level between 5 and 10 percent U-235. This was also the first time that any U.S. commercial uranium enrichment facility has produced that level of enrichment, according to the company.

The Nuclear Regulatory Commission in September granted UUSA authorization to enrich uranium up to 10 percent U-235. The company anticipates that its production of commercial quantities of LEU+ will begin by mid-2026.

LEU+ Advantages

The availability of LEU+ is expected to support:

  • Longer operating cycles for existing light water nuclear reactors
  • Potential savings in operating and maintenance costs
  • Accelerated deployment of new accident tolerant fuel designs for advanced reactors

UUSA added that LEU+ can also serve as feedstock for producing high-assay low-enriched uranium (HALEU), which is uranium that has been enriched to between 10 and 20 percent U-235.

New Centrifuge Cascade

UUSA's third new centrifuge cascade began production of LEU on December 16 at the company's National Enrichment Facility in New Mexico. The company's goal is to install 700,000 separative work units (SWU) of capacity by 2027.

According to UUSA, each of the three new cascades that were installed in 2025 began operation ahead of schedule, demonstrating the company's ability to add new enrichment capacity to meet the growing demands of the U.S. market and support the U.S. government's efforts to eliminate reliance on imported Russian enriched uranium.
