IAEA launches nuclear fuel supply chain competition for young professionals

The International Atomic Energy Agency is inviting young professionals under 35 years of age to submit essays on innovations in the nuclear fuel supply chain, covering topics from uranium exploration to the recycling of spent nuclear fuel. Nuclear trivia—reveal

The answer: Since 1949, 52 reactors have been built and operated on INL’s 890-square-mile site. It would not be quite accurate to say, however, that those 52 reactors have been built at INL. As we covered in a previous Throwback Thursday, the INL site has born many names since the establishment of the Naval Proving Ground in 1942. Some history: In 1949, the Atomic Energy Commission chose the Naval Proving Ground for the new National Reactor Testing Station. In 1951, the first reactor at NRTS came on line: Experimental Breeder Reactor-1, which famously produced the world’s first usable electricity from nuclear energy. EBR-1’s achievement was one of the first—but far from the last—massive nuclear milestones achieved at the INL site. Shortly after EBR-1 came the Materials Testing Reactor, Boiling Water Reactor Experiment No. 1, EBR-II, and many more. As the reactor campus at NRTS became known as Argonne West, and then was officially renamed INEL, then INEEL, and finally INL, still more ambitious and groundbreaking projects came, firmly establishing the site as one of the most important places in the nuclear world. Today, four of the 52 reactors at INL are still operational: the Advanced Test Reactor, the Advanced Test Reactor Critical Facility, the Transient Reactor Test Facility, and the Neutron Radiography Reactor. Looking to tomorrow: New nuclear momentum continues to carry INL forward too. Most recently, it has been the prime location for companies involved in the Department of Energy’s Reactor Pilot Program. Both Oklo and Aalo are breaking ground at the site for their Aurora and Aalo-X reactors, respectively. Go deeper: To read a brief history of each of the 52 reactors from INL, click here.
