TBT: Atoms for Peace, 72 years later

On December 8, 1952, President Dwight D. Eisenhower, delivered his historic “Atoms for Peace” speech to the United Nations General Assembly in New York City. This moment in our shared nuclear history is widely looked at as both a tipping point in favor of the peaceful use of nuclear power and as one of the most important moments in the story of nuclear. With the anniversary of that speech passing earlier this week, we have gathered a wide variety of writings from across our archives that look at the speech through many different lenses and perspectives. The speech itself: First, Nuclear News’s Eisenhower’s “Atoms for Peace” at 70 (2023) gives a succinct description of the historical moment in which Eisenhower was speaking and a broad description of the changes it catalyzed. This article also includes the transcript of the speech in its entirety. Words from Huff: Also that year, Kathryn Huff further reflected in the “Leaders” column of the December issue of NN (p. 12; members only) on the speech’s importance with an eye to how Eisenhower’s words contained prescient guidance that still apply today’s energy sector. Further back: Looking deeper into the NN archive, two issues stand out as presenting particularly thorough and multifaceted examinations of Eisenhower’s speech. The November 2003 issue of NN contains the “Atoms for Peace Special Section” (pp. 37–56). It again begins with the transcript of the speech before transitioning to statements by 11 leaders from the nuclear sector, who each give a unique perspective on the importance and legacy of the speech. Finally, the January 2004 issue of NN features a meeting report on an embedded topical at 2003’s ANS Winter Conference & Expo: “Global 2003—Atoms for Prosperity: Updating Eisenhower’s Global Vision for Nuclear Energy” (pp. 55–57).
