Blades-in turbine inspections at Quad Cities set new benchmark for Constellation

When Constellation decided to install replacement Alstom low-pressure turbines at three of its boiling water reactor plants more than 15 years ago, one benefit was knowing the new turbines should operate reliably—and without major inspections—for several years. When time finally came around to do preventative maintenance that called for a thorough inspection of the last-stage blades of the turbine rotors, Constellation pursued two paths: The standard service method: removing each blade to check for stress-corrosion cracking. An alternative procedure first validated by the Electric Power Research Institute: phased array ultrasonic testing (PAUT). Using the latter method, blades can stay in place while ultrasonic nondestructive examination (NDE) is used to scan and inspect the blades and their “root and steeple” attachment area. Expanding use: Constellation decided to implement PAUT for its Alstom turbines, starting with Quad Cities-2. There, during a refueling outage in spring 2024, the method saved five days on the outage critical path schedule. That initial project led the way for similar work at at Quad Cities-1, Dresden-2, and Peach Bottom-3. Over a total of six outages at three plants—with only the Dresden-3 and Peach Bottom-2 inspections yet to perform—cost and labor savings could amount to over $50 million and 18 critical path days. NRC proposes rule changes in response to EO

In April, President Donald Trump signed Executive Order 14270, “Zero-Based Regulatory Budgeting to Unleash American Energy,” which calls for the Nuclear Regulatory Commission to issue a “sunset rule” to effectively nullify certain regulations in no more than five years. In response, on December 3, the NRC proposed to amend its regulations to insert conditional sunset dates into several obsolete regulations. Go deeper on Nuclear NewsWire.

Cesium-137 irradiators exit Oklahoma

The Department of Energy’s NNSA reported that it has removed all cesium-137 irradiators from the state of Oklahoma. Irradiators are used for medical applications such as blood treatment and cancer research. However, many of them contain cesium-137, a radioactive material that poses a risk if stolen and used to produce a radiological dispersal device, also called a dirty bomb.
