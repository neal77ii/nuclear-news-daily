Constellation, Walmart sign PPA for Dresden

On Monday, Constellation announced that it has entered into a long-term nuclear power purchase agreement (PPA) for 176 MW of electricity from its Dresden nuclear power plant. That PPA, which will also help facilitate a 30-MWe uprate, is providing power to an unusual partner: Walmart. Go deeper on Nuclear Newswire.

New fusion initiative begins in Germany

Two German institutions—the University of Rostock and the Helmholtz-Zentrum Dresden-Rossendorf research center—have come together to launch HEDI: The High Energy Density Initiative. The initiative will serve as a fusion research hub to investigate the physical processes and extreme conditions associated with nuclear fusion. Go deeper on Nuclear Newswire.

From theory to reality

Recently, I spent an afternoon at Kairos Power’s Hermes construction site in Oak Ridge, Tenn. What I saw there was not a rendering, not a licensing presentation, not another panel about the future of advanced nuclear. I saw dozens of pilings built to NQA-1 standards. I saw seismic isolators ready for installation. I saw heavy equipment moving dirt and crews preparing concrete and steel. Read ANS CEO Craig Piercy’s full column on Nuclear Newswire.

TBT: The first CANDU export

When Canada began working on its CANDU reactor in the late 1950s, it quickly developed into the 22-MWe Nuclear Power Demonstration, which came on line in 1962, and the 200-MWe Douglas Point, which came on line in 1968. (The full story of these early reactors was the subject of a previous TBT.) Despite being in the most nascent stages of its nuclear power sector, by the end of the 1960s, Canada was already engaging with several countries about the prospect of deploying CANDUs internationally. The first of those countries to have their imported CANDU enter commercial operation was Pakistan, where Karachi nuclear power plant (KANUPP) achieved that milestone in October 1972. KANUPP was a 125-MWe reactor sited on the coast of the Arabian Sea. The project was financed by the Canadian International Development Agency; designed, supplied, and constructed by Canadian General Electric; and technically supported by Atomic Energy of Canada. As Pakistan’s first foray into nuclear power production, KANUPP faced numerous issues throughout its early years. One example of those problems, according to a 1990 Nuclear News article, was that in its first two years of operations KANUPP drew “tons and tons of sardines” into its pumphouse. This problem was solved by having local fishermen “fish the area out.” Going it alone: Things got more difficult for KANUPP and the Pakistan Atomic Energy Commission (PAEC) in 1976, when Pakistan refused to sign a nonproliferation treaty or accept IAEA inspections at its nuclear facilities. In response, the government of Canada directed Canadian companies to no longer support the plant, withdrew its technical assistance, and stopped supplying fuel and spare parts to the country. Reflecting on this sudden change, S. M. N. Zaidi, a representative of PAEC, said to NN, “All of a sudden we found that—my God—we were running the plant ourselves.” Naturally, Zaidi characterized this sudden change as a “tremendous challenge” that caused significant shortages in spare parts in fuel. However, Munir Ahmad Khan, then PAEC chairman, told NN that those “lean years” were also good ones. “It forced us to be on our feet and to learn.” In fact, despite these early difficulties, Pakistan managed to keep Karachi-1 on line until 2021. Today, PAEC operates six other commercial power reactors, two at Karachi and four at its second plant, Chashma. Go deeper: Read a full reflection on the early days of KANUPP and Pakistan’s nuclear sector in the March 1990 issue of Nuclear News. (p. 38–45) (members only)
